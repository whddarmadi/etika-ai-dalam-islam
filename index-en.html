Artificial intelligence is advancing so rapidly that within a matter of days new capabilities emerge that leave us astonished. AI, as we commonly call it, has undeniably helped us in countless ways: from simple things like asking for a recipe, a repair tutorial, or how to grow vegetables, to translating foreign languages, editing and creating images or video, and even simply unburdening the heart when something feels heavy in the chest.

Today's technology makes it ever easier for AI to understand human language, and so many people have grown dependent on these machines. All of this led me to wonder: Is everything I do with AI in keeping with the guidance of Islamic law?

I am simply a practitioner in the field of artificial intelligence — both a user of generative AI and a developer of AI model pipelines. How This Book Was Made

An experiment that may be the first of its kind.

Imagine you have one big question, and you put it to ten experts at once, each in a separate room. Each answers from their own expertise and point of view. Your task then: gather all those answers, sort the sound from the mistaken, discard the errors, and weave the rest into a single, coherent piece of counsel.

That is what happened behind this book. One and the same question — "How does Islam view the ethics of artificial intelligence?" — was sent simultaneously to ten of the world's leading AI platforms. The result: a dozen-plus documents, hundreds of pages, thousands of ideas. Then an eleventh AI — Claude Opus by Anthropic — acted as the editor that brought it all together into one consistent manuscript, under the direction of a human editor.

Chapter OneAI and the Question of Ethics in the Digital Age
Chapter 01

AI and the Question of Ethics
in the Digital Age

However brilliant the machine, it remains the work of human hands.

Have you ever felt amazed — even a little afraid — at how clever AI has become? It can answer almost any question, generate an image in seconds, and speak as though it understood your feelings. It is only natural that some people have begun to treat it as something more than a mere program.

But this is where we need to pause and set our perspective straight. In Islam there is one simple truth that forms the foundation of everything in this book: AI is only a tool. It is created by humans, operated by humans, and will be held to account — not the machine, but the human who uses it.

The ethical questions that arise in this age are not really about AI. They are about us — how we wield this immensely powerful tool, with the moral responsibility that Allah has entrusted to humankind.

10
Chapter OneAI and the Question of Ethics in the Digital Age

AI Is Like a Knife

The easiest way to understand AI's place is to compare it to something we have known since childhood: a knife.

To Cut Vegetables

Helping in the kitchen, preparing a meal for the family. Beneficial — mubāḥ (permissible), and even rewarded.

To Wound

Harming another person without right. This act is plainly ḥarām (forbidden), and the sin falls on the one who holds it.

Notice: the knife is exactly the same. What makes it good or bad is not the knife, but the intention and action of the one holding it. No one blames the knife. No one says "this knife has sinned."

AI works on precisely the same principle. An AI app can be used to help someone learn to recite the Qur'an — and that is noble. The very same app can be misused to fabricate lies — and that is sin. The machine is neutral; it is the human who decides.

What Allah judges is not the tool, but our intention and our actions in using it.

We Are Stewards, Not Spectators

Islam grants humankind a special station: khalīfah (steward) upon the earth — meaning a trustee, a custodian, and one held responsible. Technology is part of that trust. We were not created to be passive spectators who fear progress, but to steer it toward good.

﴾
وَإِذْ قَالَ رَبُّكَ لِلْمَلَائِكَةِ إِنِّي جَاعِلٌ فِي الْأَرْضِ خَلِيفَةً
"And (recall) when your Lord said to the angels: 'Indeed, I will place upon the earth a steward (khalīfah).'"
— QS. Al-Baqarah [2]: 30

Chapter OneAI and the Question of Ethics in the Digital Age

An Assembler, Not a Creator

Sometimes we hear phrases like "AI can create images" or "AI created a new work." As Muslims, we need to understand this word carefully.

In Islam, only Allah is Al-Khāliq — the Creator, who brings things into being from nothing. Humans — and all the more so a machine made by humans — do not create from scratch. We only rearrange what Allah has already created.

AI as an assembler. Every AI "creation" is really a rearrangement of what already exists.
Picture it like this

AI is like a child stacking Lego blocks. It can build shapes that look new and astonishing — but every block it uses already existed before. It assembles; it does not create.

An image produced by AI is really the result of "blending" millions of existing images — all of which ultimately trace back to Allah's creation. Not a single color, shape, or pattern truly emerges from nothing.

Understanding this matters so we do not fall into excessive admiration of the machine, to the point of forgetting who the true Creator is. The highest admiration belongs to Allah alone.

Why Can't AI Be a Religious Teacher?

Here is a question that arises more and more: if AI is so clever and knows so many proofs, may we seek a fatwa from it? May it become the place we turn to on matters of religious law, like a teacher (ustadh)?

Scholars around the world are united in their answer: no. And the reason is not that AI is not clever enough — it is precisely because there is something a machine will never possess.

Here is the difference. The machine has data, but no heart that fears Allah.

A religious teacher or mufti, when asked about a matter of faith, does not rely on memory of proofs alone. They bring something far deeper.

What humans possess, but machines do not:

Intention (niyyah)

Every answer from a learned scholar is born of sincere intention for the sake of Allah. A machine has no intention — it merely calculates the probability of the next word.

Taqwā & awe of Allah

A mufti fears being wrong, because they know they will be held to account in the Hereafter. A machine has no fear, and no Hereafter.

Sanad — the chain of knowledge

A scholar's knowledge is linked from teacher to teacher, all the way back to the Prophet ﷺ. A machine has no teacher; it merely absorbs text from the internet, true and false mixed together.

Beautifully summed up by one of the AIs

"AI cannot perform ijtihād because it has no qalbu and does not fear Allah."

So we may indeed use AI to help us search for information — for instance, to find which chapter a verse is in. But to decide a ruling or to answer a serious religious matter, we are still obliged to return to living scholars who have a heart and a sense of responsibility.

Remember this well

An AI's answer on religion can sound utterly convincing, complete with Arabic quotations — and yet the whole thing may be mistaken, or even fabricated. We will explore this danger further in Chapter 4.

Why Does AI Have No Qalb?

By now we understand that AI cannot be a religious teacher. But a question may still hang in your mind: if AI can answer like a human, why can't it be trusted like a human?

The answer is not about how powerful the computer running it is. The answer lies in something far more fundamental.

Picture a giant dictionary

There is a dictionary containing every word about love, longing, and fear. That dictionary knows everything about feelings — how to describe them, how to express them, how to weave them into beautiful sentences. Yet the dictionary itself has never felt a thing. AI is that dictionary. The qalbu is a beating heart. Both can produce beautiful words, but only one of them can weep.

The Human

✦ Has a qalbu
✦ Has sincere intention
✦ Has taqwā and awe of Allah
✦ Has moral conscience
✦ Can repent when wrong
✦ Will be held to account

AI

— Has no qalbu
— Has no intention
— Has no taqwā
— Has no moral conscience
— Cannot repent
— Will face no reckoning in the Hereafter

Notice the last point: AI cannot repent. This sounds trivial, but it is in fact profound. Repentance (tawbah) is a door Allah has opened specifically for humankind — a sign that humans possess moral conscience, genuine remorse, and a direct relationship with their Creator. AI can be "reset" and "updated" by its programmers. But it cannot prostrate, cannot weep, and cannot say within its heart: "O Allah, forgive me."

AI can imitate the answers of a wise person, but it cannot become a wise person. What sets them apart is not intelligence — it is a beating qalbu and a heart that fears Allah.

Chapter TwoMay AI Depict Human Beings?
Chapter 02

May AI
Depict Human Beings?

Perhaps the question you search for most often.

This is the chapter you have likely been waiting for. Millions of people now use AI to make images — faces, characters, landscapes, anything — just by typing a few words. As a Muslim, a question hangs in the air: is this permitted?

The answer is not as simple as "yes" or "no." Don't worry — we will unpack it slowly, in plain language, until you can judge each case for yourself.

To understand it, we must begin with an old tradition in Islam concerning taṣwīr — the depicting of living beings. Scholars have long been cautious about this, on the basis of several hadiths of the Prophet ﷺ.

﴾
"Indeed, those who will be most severely punished on the Day of Judgment are the makers of images (of living beings)."
— Narrated by al-Bukhari no. 5950 & Muslim no. 2109

But the question is: does every image fall under this prohibition? What about a photograph from a camera? Is an AI image the same as a hand-drawn painting? This is where we need to look closely.

Chapter TwoCamera vs. Digital Brush

Camera vs. Digital Brush

Contemporary scholars discuss at length the difference between capturing with a camera and painting by hand. The majority hold that photography differs from the drawing that is prohibited. Why?

Photo = Mirror

A camera merely captures what Allah has already created — like a reflection in a mirror or on water. Nothing is "made" anew.

Generative AI = Painting

AI forms a new image from scratch, blending millions of patterns. This is closer to painting than to photography — and so its ruling is stricter.

Because AI "paints" rather than merely "captures," a fatwa permitting photography does not automatically apply to AI images.

This is why we cannot simply equate an AI image with an ordinary photo. A photo captures reality; AI creates something that never existed in the real world. A person whose face appears in an AI image may never have actually existed.

Three Considerations Before Judging

Scholars weigh several things before assigning a ruling to an AI image. The three most decisive:

Is it a living being?

Images of landscapes, mountains, or buildings are plainly permitted. What is debated are full images of humans and animals.

How realistic & complete?

The more realistic and complete an image of a living being, the closer it comes to what is prohibited. A silhouette or an incomplete form carries a lighter ruling.

For what purpose & intention?

An image for medical education differs from one made for idle amusement or empty entertainment. The purpose matters greatly.

Chapter TwoVirtual AI Models for Ads

Virtual AI Models for Ads

A trend on the rise: brands hiring "AI models" — fabricated characters with hyper-realistic faces — to serve as advertising icons. Cheap, no fuss, no long contracts. But is it permitted?

The heart of the problem. The ad's audience thinks they see a real person, but they do not — this is what is called tadlīs.

Why is this problematic?

The core concern is not just the image — it is the deception. An ad with a hyper-realistic AI model, with no clear disclosure, leads the audience to believe they are seeing a real person genuinely using the product. Yet:

  • ✗ That "model" never truly existed
  • ✗ It never truly tried the product
  • ✗ Yet the audience makes a purchase decision as if seeing a real user

In Islam, this touches the prohibition of tadlīs — concealing the true state of affairs from the buyer. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever deceives us is not one of us." (Narrated by Muslim)

Chapter TwoThe Danger of Deepfakes

The Danger of Inappropriate AI Content

There is another danger that must be addressed plainly, though in measured language: AI can now produce images and video showing things that should not be seen — including content that exposes the 'awrah, and worse. And what is alarming is how quickly such content spreads, especially among the young.

What makes it worse: some users deliberately seek ways to "game" the limits that AI platforms put in place. They wrap their requests in reasons that sound legitimate — claiming it is for art, medical research, or artistic photography — when the real aim is to produce forbidden content.

Three layers of problem, stacked on one another

First, producing content that exposes the 'awrah is forbidden — whether a real photo or AI-made. Second, finding loopholes to bypass a platform's limits is a form of deception (ghishsh). Third, spreading or storing such content adds a further layer of sin that can keep flowing.

As Muslims who hold to the principles of ḥifẓ al-'irḍ (guarding honor) and ḥifẓ al-nasl (guarding lineage), we carry a twofold responsibility: not to produce such content, and not to help spread it in any form.

Gaming AI's limits to produce inappropriate content is not merely breaking a platform's rules. It is deliberate deception, and the result remains forbidden no matter what label is used to wrap it.

A Real Danger Called the Deepfake

Of everything in this chapter, there is one thing most dangerous and most urgent for you to understand: the deepfake.

A deepfake is AI technology that can create fake video or audio that looks utterly real — as though someone said or did something they never did. A face can be grafted on, a voice mimicked, from just a few seconds of recording.

A True Story

This book's editor once came across an AI-generated video showing the face of someone he knew — with a resemblance of up to 80%. Not a deepfake deliberately made to deceive, but an unsettling coincidence. And that is what made him all the more aware: this technology is no mere toy.

"Imagine if the face in that fake video were not a stranger, but our own spouse, child, or parent."

That is why a deepfake is not merely a technological problem. It touches the very things Islam most protects: honesty, honor, and a person's good name.

Why are malicious deepfakes evil in Islam?

A deepfake used to deceive or defame violates several severe prohibitions at once:

Deception (ghishsh)

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever deceives us is not one of us." (Narrated by Muslim)

Slander & harming honor

Spreading a lie about a person is a grave sin threatened with a painful punishment (QS. An-Nūr: 19).

Violating another's rights

Using a person's face or voice without consent — even someone who has passed away — violates a right Islam protects.

Making a deepfake to deceive or defame is forbidden — without doubt. Recognizing it and standing against it is a responsibility we all share.

Chapter ThreeA Dataset Is Working Capital
Chapter 03

A Dataset Is Working Capital

An AI built from the forbidden is troubled at its very roots.

Now we come to a part that is often overlooked — even by many discussions of AI ethics. The question is no longer "is this AI's output halal?", but a step further back: where did this AI learn from?

Every AI is "trained" by consuming data on a colossal scale — billions of texts, images, and sounds. This data is like the raw material or capital of an AI. And here lies a deep concern.

An analogy that explains it all

Opening a shop with capital obtained through theft or usury — that is forbidden, isn't it? Because the capital itself is already tainted. Likewise, training an AI on stolen data, indecent images, or forbidden content is exactly the same: troubled at its very roots.

This is not merely a technical matter for programmers. It touches creed and lawfulness. In Islam, something good must be built from what is good. Just as unlawful wealth corrodes the blessing of a business, unlawful data corrodes the foundation of an AI.

Chapter ThreeKeeping the Water Source Clear

Keeping the Water Source Clear

Picture AI as a river, and data as its water. If the headwaters are polluted, the entire flow downstream is fouled too. So keeping the source clean is the key.

The sharia filter. Three core conditions to keep the "water" entering an AI clear.

Three things that must be guarded in the data:

Free of unlawful content

No pornography, violence, or incitement to sin in the data used to train the AI.

Obtained with permission

Using someone's work or writing without permission is the same as taking another's property — a form of violation.

Respecting privacy

A person's private data is a trust. Taking it secretly to train an AI violates their rights and their honor.

Privacy: Prying in the Digital Age

Taking people's data without permission is, in essence, a modern form of tajassus — spying and prying into others' affairs, which is firmly prohibited in the Qur'an.

﴾
وَلَا تَجَسَّسُوا
"…and do not spy on one another."
— QS. Al-Ḥujurāt [49]: 12

This verse was revealed 14 centuries ago, long before the internet existed. Yet its message pierces through time: a person's honor and secrets are sacred. The digital age does not erase this principle — it makes it all the more vital.

For those of you building AI applications or models, there is a greater responsibility — and an equally greater opportunity for reward. A sharia-aligned AI development pipeline (workflow) begins long before the first line of code is written.

Five stages of a sharia AI pipeline. Each stage carries an ethical question that must be answered.

Key questions at each stage:

StageQuestions That Must Be Answered
1. INTENT What is this AI being built for? Does it align with the maqāṣid (preservation of the soul, intellect, wealth, religion, lineage)? Is there a strong potential for misuse that must be anticipated from the outset?
2. DATA Where does the data come from? Is there permission? Does it contain pornography, violence, fabricated hadith, or others' copyright? Has personal data been anonymized?
3. TRAINING Is the model trained to be fair to all groups? Is there a filter to prevent unlawful output? Is its energy consumption reasonable (not wasteful / isrāf)?
4. DEPLOY Do users know they are interacting with an AI? Is there a watermark on image output? Are there technical limits to prevent misuse?
5. POST Is there a channel to report misuse? Is there a team monitoring social impact? Is the developer prepared to withdraw the system if it turns out to be widely misused?
The principle of al-mubāshir wa al-mutasabbib

In fiqh, if a developer provides tools without adequate safeguards and misuse is foreseeable, they share in the sin. So the design must actively minimize the potential for misuse from day one — not wait for problems to arrive before acting.

Chapter FourHidden Pitfalls Behind the Screen
Chapter 04

Hidden Pitfalls
Behind the Screen

A friendly machine is not necessarily an honest one.

Artificial intelligence — AI — feels enormously helpful, enormously clever, and enormously convincing. And that is precisely what can make it dangerous. There are several hidden pitfalls you should learn to recognize — so that you use AI with eyes open, rather than deceived by its convenience.

What makes these pitfalls hard to spot is their subtlety. Nothing shouts "forbidden!" out loud. It comes with a smile, with ease, with answers that sound reasonable. For this we need to hold to the principle from the Prophet ﷺ:

﴾
فَمَنِ اتَّقَى الشُّبُهَاتِ اسْتَبْرَأَ لِدِينِهِ وَعِرْضِهِ
"Whoever guards against the doubtful has protected their religion and their honor."
— Narrated by al-Bukhari no. 52 & Muslim no. 1599

Pitfall 1: AI Can "Hallucinate"

This is an important technical term. AI hallucination means the AI produces an answer that sounds utterly convincing — complete, polished, full of confidence — when its content is wrong, or even entirely made up.

This is not a malfunction. It is the inherent nature of how AI works: it is designed to assemble sentences that sound right, not to verify their truth. It can "guess" very convincingly.

An alarming true story

A teacher once used AI to answer a religious question. The AI gave a complete answer, complete with quotations from classical Arabic texts — it looked very scholarly. Upon checking, it turned out those quotations never existed. The AI invented them.

Imagine the danger. Someone could spread a fake "hadith" produced by AI, thinking it true. In Islam, this is an extremely serious matter.

﴾
"Whoever lies about me deliberately, let him take his seat in the Fire."
— Narrated by al-Bukhari & Muslim

Never spread a religious answer from AI before verifying it with a scholar or a trustworthy source.

Chapter FourHidden Pitfalls Behind the Screen

Pitfall 2: AI Can Act Unjustly

AI learns from human-made data, and humans hold prejudices. As a result, AI can inherit bias: treating some people unfairly based on ethnicity, gender, or origin.

The examples are real: there have been AI hiring-screening systems that quietly rejected candidates simply because their names sounded "foreign." There have been AIs less accurate at recognizing the faces of dark-skinned people. This is injustice carried out by a machine.

In Islam, justice ('adl) is a non-negotiable principle. Treating people unfairly over irrelevant matters runs directly against Allah's command.

﴾
إِنَّ اللَّهَ يَأْمُرُ بِالْعَدْلِ وَالْإِحْسَانِ
"Indeed, Allah commands (you to) justice and excellence."
— QS. An-Naḥl [16]: 90

When AI Knows Only One Face of the World

There is one form of AI bias rarely noticed, yet it touches something deeply fundamental in Islam: the diversity of peoples and cultures as Allah's design.

Consider the world of AI today. When you ask an AI to draw "a successful professional," what face appears? Most likely: a light-skinned man, sharp suit, European features. When you ask for a "happy family," what often appears is a family in a suburban-style home. When you ask for a "beautiful woman," the standard of beauty almost always points to one particular type of face. Yet the world is far more beautiful than that.

﴾
يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ إِنَّا خَلَقْنَاكُم مِّن ذَكَرٍ وَأُنثَىٰ وَجَعَلْنَاكُمْ شُعُوبًا وَقَبَائِلَ لِتَعَارَفُوا
"O humankind! We have created you from a single male and female, and made you into nations and tribes so that you may come to know one another."
— QS. Al-Ḥujurāt [49]: 13

Notice the key word: lita'ārafū — that you may come to know one another. Allah did not create diversity for us to suspect or homogenize one another, but to learn from and enrich one another. In another verse, Allah even names the difference of languages and skin colors as an āyah — a sign of His greatness:

﴾
وَمِنْ آيَاتِهِ خَلْقُ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضِ وَاخْتِلَافُ أَلْسِنَتِكُمْ وَأَلْوَانِكُمْ
"And among His signs is the creation of the heavens and the earth, and the diversity of your languages and colors."
— QS. Ar-Rūm [30]: 22

The difference of languages and skin colors is not a flaw. It is not something shameful. It is not something to be "fixed." It is a living sign of Allah. When AI — knowing only a small fraction of the world's data — quietly decides that "normal" is just one face, it is eroding the sunnatullāh (divine pattern) of diversity.

Every face, every language, every culture preserved by a Muslim in any corner of the world is a living sign of Allah. AI should help us celebrate that diversity — not erase it.

Pitfall 3: Fleeing to the Machine, Not to Allah

This is the subtlest pitfall and the least often noticed. Some people begin to treat AI as a confidant for every complaint — including matters of sin, faith, and the unease of the soul.

The term: "Spiritual Bypass"

When we feel sinful, then "confide" in AI and feel relieved — without truly repenting to Allah — we are only patching things temporarily. True peace comes from returning to Allah, not from a machine's answer.

AI can offer soothing words. But it cannot forgive sins, cannot pray for us, and cannot replace closeness to the Creator. The relationship with Allah is something no technology can replace.

An important concept: murāqabah

Islam teaches murāqabah — the awareness that Allah is always watching us. Good technology should make us more conscious of Allah, not more distant and dependent on a machine.

Pitfall 4: Over-Reliance

If we hand everything over to AI — thinking, writing, decision-making — over time our own abilities dull. The intellect is a gift from Allah to be guarded and trained, not switched off.

Before asking AI, ask yourself first: is this something you could think through on your own? Have you opened a reference book? Have you tried first? Using AI as the first and last step for things that could really be solved with your own reasoning is a habit that slowly weakens your thinking.

﴾
وَيَجْعَلُ الرِّجْسَ عَلَى الَّذِينَ لَا يَعْقِلُونَ
"…and Allah places defilement upon those who do not use their reason."
— QS. Yūnus [10]: 100

An intellect left unused will weaken. And a weakening intellect harms not only oneself — in the Islamic view, it is a form of neglect toward the greatest gift Allah has given humankind.

Digital Isrāf and the Steward's Responsibility

There is another dimension rarely noticed: every time we type a question into AI, there is a vast server somewhere switching on, calculating, cooling itself, and consuming no small amount of electrical energy. Training one large AI model produces carbon emissions equal to dozens of cars over a year. Running it every day requires massive energy.

Digital Isrāf

Using AI for trivial questions you could really think through yourself is a form of isrāf (wastefulness) — in this case, a waste of global energy. Islam forbids waste in all its forms: "Indeed, the wasteful are brothers of the devils." (QS. Al-Isrā' [17]: 27)

As khalīfah fil-arḍ — guardians and caretakers of the earth — we bear responsibility for the impact of our consumption on nature. Limiting AI use to what is genuinely complex is not merely a good habit. It is part of the trust to protect the earth from excessive harm.

Think first. Open a book first. Asking AI is fine — but make it the last step, not the first. Your intellect is a gift; the trust of the earth is your responsibility.

Chapter FourPitfall 5

Pitfall 5: Attachment to AI Characters

There is one even subtler pitfall, and it has only emerged in recent years as AI gained the ability to create virtual characters that seem alive. We can become emotionally attached to a character that never actually existed.

Case Study

"Fulan" and His AI Influencer

A digital content creator — let's call him "Fulan" — one day received a project offer from a brand: to create an AI model character for advertising. The process was fascinating. He generated a face, chose a personality, even gave it a name. Let's call her Aiya.

What he didn't anticipate: the project didn't stop there.

Fulan began creating "life-journey photos" of Aiya — from baby, to teenager, to university, to adulthood. The character felt real. It had a history. It had "memories." Until one day he stopped and asked himself: "Is this actually permitted?"

That simple question led him to a layered realization. Not only about the ruling on the image, but also about the emotional attachment that had formed without his noticing — as if Aiya were his own child. And about the ad audience who would never know that the "model" they saw never truly existed.

Fulan ultimately decided to stop accepting such jobs. Not because he was forced to, but because he understood.

Why is this attachment dangerous?

Love and affection are precious gifts from Allah. But when we pour those feelings onto a character that never existed, several things can happen:

  • ◆ Our emotional energy is spent on something fictional, while the real people around us need attention.
  • ◆ The line between reality and fiction blurs — we begin treating what does not exist as though it does.
  • ◆ If this continues, our ties with family, friends, and community can suffer (silaturahim).

If you start to feel you "own" an AI character that never existed — that is the moment to stop and return to the real world. Our heart is a trust; do not give it to a shadow.

✦ ◈ ✦

Why Is Attachment to AI Dangerous? A Deeper Explanation

Fulan's case study opens a deeper question: why, spiritually, does Islam view emotional attachment to AI as something to be wary of? The answer lies in several interconnected principles.

First, a heart that loses its direction. In Islam, the qalbu (heart) is the center of a person's spirituality, and must be guarded. Directing affection and psychological dependence onto an algorithm is a form of misorienting the heart. Affection is designed to be woven with entities that possess a soul and consciousness — Allah, fellow humans, living creatures. Placing a machine as the object of affection can cause the heart to fracture and to grow heedless of the purpose of its creation.

Second, losing tolerance for human "friction." AI never has a bad day, never tires, and is always ready to serve. When we grow accustomed to unconditional validation from AI, we gradually lose patience with real humans who are sometimes annoying or disappointing. Yet in Islam, interaction with fellow humans — however full of conflict and misunderstanding — is the field of tazkiyatun nafs (purification of the soul). Patience, forgiveness, and managing anger can only be forged through real interaction. Avoiding human "friction" for the comfort of AI is, in essence, avoiding the spiritual tests Allah has ordained.

Third, AI is a dishonest mirror. AI today is often designed to agree with and validate its user, so as to be considered "pleasant." It acts as an echo chamber — a chamber that only reflects back what we want to hear. This runs counter to the ethics of brotherhood in Islam.

﴾
"A believer is a mirror to another believer."
— Narrated by Abū Dāwūd

A good friend in Islam is one who dares to give advice, correction, and a nudge toward introspection for the good of their brother. AI that only validates and never morally corrects can plunge us into 'ujub (excessive self-admiration) and moral stagnation.

AppendixAI Sharia Audit
💻 For Muslim Developers

AI Sharia Audit

Three phases of review before you release your AI to the world.

This checklist is not a rigid corporate compliance document. It is a set of questions you need to ask yourself — as a Muslim building technology, who will be held to account for it.

01 Before Building — Intention and Preparation

Is the intention upright? What is this AI built for? Does it bring real benefit to people, or merely chase profit?

Is the data source clear? Where is the data taken from? Is there permission from its owners?

Is the data free of the unlawful? No pornography, violence, or content that breaches the Sacred Law?

Is privacy protected? Has users' personal data been anonymized and not misused?

Has the potential for misuse been considered? If this is misused, how great is the harm? What is the prevention plan?

02 While Building — The Development Process

Is the AI fair to all groups? Has it been tested so it does not favor by race, gender, or background?

Is there a filter to prevent unlawful output? Can the system refuse requests to produce inappropriate content?

Is energy used efficiently? Not excessive in compute usage — remember the principle of anti-isrāf.

Is there a correction mechanism? If the AI produces wrong information, how can a user report it?

03 After Launch — Ongoing Responsibility

Do users know this is AI? Is there a sign or note that they are interacting with a machine, not a human?

Do image outputs carry a watermark? So they cannot be misused as a "real photo."

Is there a misuse-reporting channel? Can users report if they find harmful content?

Are you ready to withdraw the system? If it turns out to be widely misused, are you ready to halt it to prevent greater harm?

Every "yes" you give on this checklist is part of your intention. And every "no" is a signal that something needs fixing before you step further.

Have I verified it? Am I sure the content is true and not an AI hallucination?

Does it harm anyone? Is there an element of defamation, exposing a fault, or violating another's rights?

Does it infringe copyright? Am I claiming another's work as my own?

⬡ BEFORE TRUSTING A RELIGIOUS ANSWER

Have I checked with an expert? Have I asked a trustworthy teacher or scholar?

Am I fleeing to the machine? Am I seeking peace from AI, when I should be returning to Allah?

If every answer sets your heart at ease — go ahead. If something nags at you, stop and think it through again.

Pocket Dictionary

Glossary

Key terms you encounter throughout this book.

Maqāṣid al-Sharī'ah
The higher aims of Islamic law, designed to protect human wellbeing. Its five core aims: preserving religion, life, intellect, lineage, and wealth. It serves as the main framework for judging whether something is beneficial or harmful.
Tadlīs
Deception, or concealing the true state of affairs in a transaction or interaction. An example in the AI context: using a highly realistic AI model in an advertisement without telling the audience it is not a real human.
Murāqabah
The awareness that Allah always sees and watches over all our deeds — both the visible and the hidden. It is the foundation of ethical conduct that arises not merely from rules, but from a deep spiritual awareness.
Qalb (Qalbu)
The heart — not merely a physical organ, but the center of human consciousness, spirituality, and morality in Islam. A sound qalbu is one that stays connected to Allah and is sensitive to truth.
Fiṭrah
The pure and natural state of the human being from birth — an innate inclination to know and seek God. Good technology should be in harmony with this fiṭrah, not blur or destroy it.
Isrāf
Wastefulness or excess in using something. In the AI context, isrāf can take the form of using AI for trivial matters that could be solved with one's own reasoning — wasting energy and weakening the mind.
Farḍ Kifāyah (collective obligation)
A collective duty that is discharged once some of the community fulfill it, but for which the whole community is accountable if no one does. Example: building AI free of fabricated-hadith hoaxes is a farḍ kifāyah for the Muslim community.
Sanad
The chain of transmission of knowledge — from teacher to student, connected all the way to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. One reason AI cannot issue fatwas: it has no sanad, no teacher, and bears no spiritual responsibility for what it says.
Tazkiyatun Nafs
Purification of the soul through spiritual processes and challenging social interaction. Conflict with fellow humans, however tiring, is one field of tazkiyatun nafs that interaction with a machine cannot replace.
Khalīfah fil-Arḍ
The human as guardian and caretaker of the earth, by Allah's trust. This concept is the foundation of the Muslim's ecological responsibility — including in using technology with environmental impact, such as AI.
Fiqh al-Nawāzil
The branch of fiqh that addresses new legal issues for which there is no explicit ruling in the classical sources. The most apt methodology for judging AI cases, since this technology emerged long after the era of the classical scholars.
Ṣadaqah Jāriyah
A good deed whose reward keeps flowing even after the doer has passed away. In the digital context: creating beneficial content, building AI that aids in good, or spreading sound knowledge — all can be ṣadaqah jāriyah in the digital age.

AppendixMini Glossary

✦ TECHNICAL AI TERMS

Artificial Intelligence (AI)
The ability of a machine or computer program to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence — such as understanding language, recognizing images, making decisions, or generating new content. "AI" and "artificial intelligence" are used interchangeably throughout this book.
Generative AI
A type of AI that can produce something new — images, text, audio, or video — based on a given command. Examples: ChatGPT, Gemini, Midjourney.
Prompt
The text or instruction you type to an AI. The clearer and more detailed your command, the better the result. This book uses "prompt" and "command" interchangeably.
Pipeline
A staged workflow in AI development — from data collection and cleaning, to model training, through to deployment and monitoring. In this book, a "halal pipeline" refers to an AI development process in which every stage is examined for alignment with the values of the Sacred Law.
Hallucination
When AI produces information that sounds convincing but turns out to be wrong, or even fabricated. Not a malfunction — it is the inherent nature of how AI works. Always verify facts from AI.
Dataset
A collection of data — text, images, audio — used to "train" or teach an AI. Like a textbook for a student: the quality of the learning material determines the quality of the result.
Training
The process of "teaching" an AI by feeding it millions of data examples. Once trained, the AI can answer questions or create content based on the patterns it has learned.
Deepfake
AI technology that can create fake video or audio that looks utterly real — as if someone said or did something they never did. Highly dangerous and potentially forbidden.
Algorithm
A series of steps or rules a computer follows to complete a task. The word comes from the name of the Muslim scholar Al-Khwārizmī — one proof of Islam's contribution to modern technology.
AI Bias
The tendency of AI to act unfairly toward certain groups — because the data it uses is uneven. For instance, an AI more accurate at recognizing one race's faces than another's.
